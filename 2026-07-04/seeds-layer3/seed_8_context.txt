[Summary: Analyzes the k=infinity limit of Andrews--Gordon: the combinatorial version becomes trivially a partition bijection, but the q-series limit yields a nontrivial infinite-sum identity equaling (1-q^t)/(q)_infty, which admits a two-variable generalization.]
Let $k=\infty$ in Theorem \ref{thm:AGcomb}. Clearly, the restriction 
of having difference at least $2$ at distance $k-1$ now becomes vacuous, and on the LHS
we are counting partitions of $n$ where $1$ appears at most $t-1$ times.
On the RHS, the modulus $2k+1$ is now infinite and thus vacuous.
Thus, we are left with counting partitions where $t$ does not appear as a part.
These two classes of partitions are obviously equinumerous. Indeed, looking at their complements, 
partitions of $n$ in which we \emph{do} have at least $t$ ones is in bijection
with partitions in which $t$ \emph{does} appear as a part.

Thus, the combinatorial versions of the Andrews--Gordon identities with $k=\infty$ are trivial.


However, looking at the $q$--series versions, the identities take the shape ($t\in\mathbb{Z}_{>0}\cup\{\infty\}$):
\begin{align}
\sum_{n_1,n_2,\cdots}
\dfrac{q^{n_1^2+n_2^2+\cdots\,\,\,+n_t+n_{t+1}+\cdots}}{(q)_{n_1-n_2}(q)_{n_2-n_3}\cdots }
=
\sum_{n_1,n_2,\cdots}
\dfrac{1}{(q)_{n_1}}
\left(
\prod_{1\leq i < t} q^{n_i^2}
\begin{bmatrix} n_i \\ n_{i+1} \end{bmatrix}
\prod_{j \geq t}q^{n_j^2+n_j}
\begin{bmatrix} n_j \\ n_{j+1} \end{bmatrix}
\right)
=\dfrac{1-q^t}{(q)_\infty}.
\label{eqn:InfiniteAG}
\end{align}
Note that for $t=\infty$ we take $q^\infty=0$.
This actually admits a two variable generalization which we shall prove below.

\title{The $\mathrm{A}_2$ Andrews--Gordon identities and cylindric partitions}

\author{S.\ Ole Warnaar}
\address{School of Mathematics and Physics,
The University of Queensland, Brisbane, QLD 4072, Australia}
\email{o.warnaar@maths.uq.edu.au}

\thanks{Work supported by the Australian Research Council.}

\subjclass[2020]{05A15, 05A19, 11P84, 17B65, 33D15, 81R10}

\begin{abstract}
Inspired by a number of recent papers by Corteel, Dousse, Foda, Uncu and 
Welsh on cylindric partitions and Rogers--Ramanujan-type identities, 
we obtain the $\mathrm{A}_2$ (or $\mathrm{A}_2^{(1)}$) 
analogues of the celebrated Andrews--Gordon identities.
We further prove $q$-series identities that correspond to the
infinite-level limit of the Andrews--Gordon identities
for $\mathrm{A}_{r-1}$ (or $\mathrm{A}_{r-1}^{(1)}$) for arbitrary rank $r$.
Our results for $\mathrm{A}_2$ also lead to conjectural, manifestly 
positive, combinatorial formulas for the $2$-variable generating function 
of cylindric partitions of rank $3$ and level $d$, such that $d$ is not a
multiple of $3$.

\smallskip

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} $\mathrm{A}_{r-1}^{(1)}$ branching functions, 
character formulas, cylindric partitions, Rogers--Ramanujan identities. 
\end{abstract}

\begin{remark}
There are a few things to notice about \cref{prop:mock-theta-sums}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[(i).] The first two identities might lead to formulae for $\phi(q) \pm 2\psi(q)$, as in \cite[(1.4) and (1.5)]{chen2012partition}. 
    \item[(ii).] The next two identities are just a convenient reformulation of \cref{eq:watson}, which can be derived from equations $(1_0)$ to $(3_1)$ from Watson's paper \cite{watson1937mock}.

    \item[(iv).] One can regard the left-hand sides as ``completions'' of mock theta functions (not in the sense of harmonic Maass forms), since they correspond to sums over $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ rather than $n \ge 0$ or $n \ge 1$; thus the fifth-order mock theta functions ``complete'' each other in this sense.

% Paper: kanade_russell_completing_a2_andrews_schilling_warnaar

[Summary: Introduces Rogers--Ramanujan identities as prototypes and defines the key notation (q-Pochhammer symbols, theta functions) used throughout the paper, situating the work in the landscape of partition theory and q-series.]
Characters of various structures related to the modules of affine Lie algebras, and more generally, vertex operator algebras, are a tremendously rich 
source of various kinds of combinatorial, number-theoretic and $q$-series-theoretic identities. 
The landscape of this subject is truly vast, and we shall restrict ourselves to the scenarios that lead to the identities of the Rogers--Ramanujan-type:
\begin{align}
\sum_{n\geq 0}\dfrac{q^{n^2}}{(q)_n} &= \prod_{m\geq 0}\dfrac{1}{(1-q^{5m+1})(1-q^{5m+4})}\label{eqn:RR1}\\
\sum_{n\geq 0}\dfrac{q^{n^2+n}}{(q)_n} &= \prod_{m\geq 0}\dfrac{1}{(1-q^{5m+2})(1-q^{5m+3})}\label{eqn:RR2}.
\end{align}

Here and below, we use the standard notation for $n\in\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}\cup\{\infty\}$:
\begin{align}
(a;q)_n=\prod_{0\leq t < n} (1-aq^t),
\end{align}
and simply writing $(a)_n$ when the base $q$ is understood.
We will also use the following notation:
\begin{align}
\theta(a;q)&=(a; q)_\infty(q/a; q)_\infty\\
\theta(a_1,a_2,\cdots,a_k;q)&=\theta(a_1;q)\theta(a_2;q)\cdots \theta(a_k;q).
\end{align}

Throughout this paper, we will be viewing identities such as \eqref{eqn:RR1} and \eqref{eqn:RR2} as purely formal power series 
identities. We shall refer to the periodicity in the product-side as the ``modulus'' of the identity, for instance, we will say

We conclude this section with a remark about Theorem~\ref{Thm_Main4}.
Due to the occurrence of the limit, the left-hand side does not take the
form of the usual sum-side of a Rogers--Ramanujan-type identity.
For special cases it is, however, possible to eliminate the limit.
For example, for partitions of the form $(2^r)$ we found that
\begin{equation}\label{Eq_Q2r}
P_{(2^r)}(1,q,q^2,\dots;q^{2n+\delta}) 
=\sum_{r\geq r_1\geq\dots\geq r_n\geq 0}
\frac{q^{r^2-r+r_1^2+\cdots+r_n^2+r_1+\cdots+r_n}}
{(q)_{r-r_1}(q)_{r_1-r_2}
\cdots(q)_{r_{n-1}-r_n}(q^{2-\delta};q^{2-\delta})_{r_n}}
\end{equation}
for $\delta=0,1$.
This turns the $m=2$ case of Theorem~\ref{Thm_Main4} into
\[
\sum_{r_1\geq\dots\geq r_n\geq 0}
\frac{q^{r_1^2+\cdots+r_n^2+r_1+\cdots+r_n}}
{(q)_{r_1-r_2}\cdots(q)_{r_{n-1}-r_n}(q^{2-\delta};q^{2-\delta})_{r_n}} 
=\frac{(q^{2n+2+\delta};q^{2n+2+\delta})_{\infty}}
{(q)_{\infty}}\, \cdot \theta(q;q^{2n+2+\delta}).
\]
For $\delta=1$ this is the $i=1$ case of the
Andrews--Gordon identity \eqref{Eq_AG} (with $m$ replaced by $n$). 
For $\delta=0$ it corresponds to the $i=1$ case of 
\eqref{Eq_Bressoud}.
We do not know how to generalize \eqref{Eq_Q2r}
to arbitrary rectangular shapes.
